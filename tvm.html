Introduction

In this module, we will learn about the time value of money. This will be our main tool for analysis throughout our course. Broadly, we want to be able to compare the value of money across different time periods, e.g. the value of $100 today vs next year. To do this, we will will use an interest rate \(r\). This rate will be called different names depending on the context. Some examples are the opportunity cost, investment return, yield to maturity, and cost of equity. We will generically call \(r\) a discount rate throughout this course, since it will allow us to discount or move cash flows from one time period to another. Let’s start with a simple example in the following video.

Discounting Single Cash Flows

Present & Future Values

Present value of future cash flow at time \(t\)

\[PV_{0} = \frac{FV_{t}}{\left( 1 + r \right)^{t}}\]

Present value of annuity with growth

\[\begin{aligned} PV_{0} &= \text{PM}T_{1} \times \text{ADF}\left( t,r,g \right) \\ \text{ADF}\left( t,r,g \right) &= \left\lbrack \frac{1 - \left( \frac{1 + g}{1 + r} \right)^{t}}{r - g} \right\rbrack \\ \end{aligned}\]

Future value of annuity with growth

\[\begin{aligned} FV_{t} &= \text{PM}T_{1} \times \text{ACF}\left( t,r,g \right) \\ \text{ACF}\left( t,r,g \right) &= \left\lbrack \frac{\left( 1 + r \right)^{t} - \left( 1 + g \right)^{t}}{r - g} \right\rbrack \\ \end{aligned}\]

Present value of perpetuity with growth

\[PV_{0} = \frac{\text{PM}T_{1}}{r - g}\]

APR, EAR & Converting Rates

The stated annual rate (also known as the APR) is the rate that is stated. It is a fake rate because it does not include the effects of compounding interest. It must be converted to a true rate. The APR is written as ____% per year compounded _____. You can only divide the APR by the compounding frequency (semi-annual, quarterly, weekly, etc.).

The Effective Annual Yield (EAR) is a true rate and includes the effects of compounding interest. You can NOT divide EAR by 2, 4, 12 to get the semi-annual, quarterly, monthly rate.

If your cash flows are at a different frequency then the compounding frequency, then you need to calculate the EAR and then convert the EAR into the rate that aligns with the frequency that match the cash flows.

Example:

Your bank offers you a loan of $100 at an annual rate of 12%, compounded monthly. How much interest would you pay in one year?

Note that the stated rate is 12%. This is our fake rate, i.e. APR=12%.

Since the compounded frequency is monthly, we must calculate the true monthly rate. Our generic formulas are:

\[\begin{aligned} 1 + APR = 1 + monthly\ rate \times 12 \\ 1 + EAR = \left( 1 + monthly\ rate \right)^{12} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Using the APR formula, we have

\[monthly\ rate = \frac{\text{APR}}{12} = \frac{12\%}{12} = 1\%\]

The monthly rate of 1% is a true rate that incorporates compounding of interest. We could calculate the interest paid over the course of a year (i.e. over the next 12 1-month periods) as

\[\$ 100 \times \left( 1 + 1\% \right)^{12} = \$ 112.68\]

and we see that we would pay $12.68 in interest over the course of a year. We could also calculate the EAR using the EAR formula

\[EAR = \left( 1 + monthly\ rate \right)^{12} - 1 = \left( 1 + 1\% \right)^{12} - 1 = 12.68\%\]

We could also calculate the interest paid over the course of a year as

\[\$ 100 \times \left( 1 + 12.68\% \right)^{1} = \$ 112.68\]

We see that the interest is again $12.68. We see that the monthly rate and EAR both take into compounding of interest.

Valuation

Price any asset by discounting its expected future cash flows back at an appropriate, risk adjusted discounted rate

\[Price = \sum_{t = 0}^{\infty}\frac{CF_{t}}{\left( 1 + r \right)^{t}}\]

Excel Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} PV &= CF_{0} + NPV\left( r,CF_{t} \right) \\ PV &= PV\left( r,n,PMT,FV \right) \\ FV &= FV\left( r,n,PMT,PV \right) \\ PMT &= PMT\left( r,n,PV,FV \right) \\ r &= IRR\left( \text{CF} \right) \\ r &= RATE\left( n,PMT,PV \right) \\ \end{aligned} \]

Price

Ex-dividend price is the present value today of future dividends paid at time 1, 2, 3, …

\[\begin{aligned} Price &= PV\left( \text{Cash Flows} \right) \\ &= PV\left( D_{1},D_{2},D_{3},\ldots \right) \\ &= \frac{D_{1}}{\left( 1 + r \right)^{1}} + \frac{D_{2}}{\left( 1 + r \right)^{2}} + \frac{D_{3}}{\left( 1 + r \right)^{3}} + \cdots \\ \end{aligned}\]

Note that we cannot write down the infinite summation, so we must make assumptions about the dividends to use our perpetuity formulas

Constant Growth Model

  • Assume dividends grow at a constant rate of \(g\)

  • \(D_{t + 1} = D_{t} \times \left( 1 + g \right)\)

  • Note the timing difference in our formula (price today, dividend tomorrow)

\[P_{0} = \frac{D_{1}}{r - g}\]

Differential Growth Model

  • Assume short run dividends followed by long run dividends following constant growth

  • To value stock, we need to:

    1. Estimate future dividends in the foreseeable future

    2. Estimate the future stock price when the stock becomes a constant growth stock

Compute the total present value of the estimated future dividends and future stock price at the appropriate discount rate

Intrest Rates

Center

  • Mean, average and expected return are synonymous in this section

  • Expected Return of an Asset

\[E\left\lbrack R \right\rbrack = \sum_{j = 1}^{s}{\text{Pro}b_{j} \times R_{j}}\]

  • Expected Return Portfolio

\[E\left\lbrack R_{P} \right\rbrack = \sum_{i = 1}^{n}{w_{i} \times E\left\lbrack R_{i} \right\rbrack}\]

Dispersion

  • Variance of an Asset

\[\text{Var}\left( R \right) = \sigma^{2} = \sum_{j = 1}^{s}{\text{Pro}b_{j} \times \left( R_{j} - E\left\lbrack R \right\rbrack \right)^{2}}\]

  • Variance of a Portfolio of Two Assets

\[\sigma_{P}^{2} = w_{1}^{2} \cdot \sigma_{1}^{2} + w_{2}^{2} \cdot \sigma_{2}^{2} + 2 \cdot w_{1} \cdot w_{2} \cdot \sigma_{1,2}\]

  • Standard Deviation

\[\text{SD}\left( R \right) = \sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}\left( R \right)} = \sqrt{\sigma^{2}}\]

Comovement

  • Covariance

\[\text{Cov}\left( R_{1},R_{2} \right) = \sigma_{1,2} = \sum_{j = 1}^{s}{\text{Pro}b_{j} \times \left( R_{1,j} - E\left\lbrack R_{1} \right\rbrack \right) \times \left( R_{2,j} - E\left\lbrack R_{2} \right\rbrack \right)}\]

  • Correlation

    • Bounded between -1 and 1

\[\text{Corr}\left( R_{1},R_{2} \right) = \rho_{1,2} = \frac{\sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}}\]

Conclusion

CAPM

\(R_{i} = R_{f} + \beta_{i} \times \left( R_{M} - R_{f} \right)\)

  • Required return for asset \(i\): \(R_{i}\)

  • Risk-free asset: \(R_{f}\)

  • Asset \(i\)’s beta: \(\beta_{i}\)

  • Risk Premium: \(R_{M} - R_{f}\)

Security’s \(\beta\)

\[\beta_{i} = \frac{\text{Cov}\left( R_{i},R_{M} \right)}{\text{Var}\left( R_{M} \right)} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{iM}}}{\sigma_{M}^{2}} = \frac{\sigma_{i}\sigma_{M}\rho_{\text{iM}}}{\sigma_{M}^{2}} = \frac{\sigma_{i}}{\sigma_{M}}\rho_{\text{iM}}\]

If efficient portfolio (i.e. on CML), then \(\rho_{\text{iM}} = 1\) and hence

\[\beta_{i} = \frac{\sigma_{i}}{\sigma_{M}}\rho_{\text{iM}} = \frac{\sigma_{i}}{\sigma_{M}}\]

Portfolio \(\beta_{P}\)

\[\beta_{P} = \sum_{i = 1}^{n}{w_{i} \times \beta_{i}}\]

Capital Market Line

CML: Plot \(R\) vs \(\sigma\)

  • Slope: Sharpe \(\frac{R_{M} - R_{f}}{\sigma_{M}}\)

  • An efficient portfolio falls on the CML and hence

\[\begin{aligned} R_{i} &= R_{f} + \beta_{i} \times \left( R_{M} - R_{f} \right) \\ &= R_{f} + \frac{\sigma_{i}}{\sigma_{M}} \times \left( R_{M} - R_{f} \right) \\ &= R_{f} + \sigma_{i} \times \frac{R_{M} - R_{f}}{\sigma_{M}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Security Market Line

SML: Plot \(R\) vs \(\beta\)

  • Slope: \(RP_{M} = R_{M} - R_{f}\)

  • \(\begin{aligned} R_{i} &= R_{f} + \beta_{i} \times \left( R_{M} - R_{f} \right) \\ \end{aligned}\)
